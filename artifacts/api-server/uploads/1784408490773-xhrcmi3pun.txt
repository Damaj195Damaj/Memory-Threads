FIELD JOURNAL — Dr. Elena Wren
Kestrel Hollow Expedition, Ardenmoor Highlands
Personal notebook — not for publication

---

June 2, 2025
Made camp by dusk. The drive up from Caldbridge took longer than planned — the lower switchback road past Fenmarsh was washed out again, so Tomas Reyes rerouted us along the ridge track. Tomas has guided three expeditions through this valley before ours and knows every goat path in it. Marcus was quiet most of the drive, nose in the 1487 monastery ledger transcript, muttering about the "fox and lantern" line again. I still think it's a boundary marker, not a riddle, but I've learned not to argue with Marcus before coffee.

Camp is set on the flat shelf above the hollow, maybe 400 meters from where the old chapel foundation should be. Weather held today — cool, overcast, no rain. Good omen or none at all.

June 3, 2025
First full day on site. We opened Trench A against the north wall of the collapsed chapel. Marcus found a bronze seal in the topsoil within the first hour — absurdly lucky. Cleaned it enough to see a rearing hare and what might be the letter "T." Logged it as KH-01. Tomas thinks the hare mark matches a family crest he's seen on a boundary stone near the stream crossing, which would fit the Thornecliff family name.

Radio contact with Caldbridge in the evening. Priya Nandan's name came up — the university office fielded a call from someone at Blackthorn Antiquities asking about our permit filing. Nothing wrong with that on its face, but Priya and I have history, and Blackthorn does not fund research out of civic spirit. I didn't mention it to Marcus. No sense worrying him yet.

June 4, 2025
Long day. Rain came in around noon and turned Trench A to soup. We found a pottery shard (KH-02) wedged under a collapsed lintel stone, red-glazed, consistent with the 15th century layer. Marcus twisted his ankle climbing out of the trench in the rain — not serious, but he's off fieldwork for a day or two. He's furious about it, mostly because he wanted to be the one to reach the chest cavity if we find it.

Tomas told a story tonight about the hollow — locals used to say the monks hid "the last true book" here before the chapel was abandoned, and that anyone who moved it without leave would lose their way home. I don't put stock in the legend, but it's a nice piece of corroborating oral history for the report.

June 5, 2025
Slower day. Surveyed the eastern slope with the ground-penetrating radar unit and got a promising anomaly about six meters from Trench A — rectangular, buried maybe 80cm down. Could be the stone chest referenced in the ledger transcript. Marked it Site B. Marcus, hobbling but stubborn, insisted on helping flag the grid.

Weather turning colder. Tomas says we have maybe four clear days before the first autumn storm system reaches the highlands, which is early even for up here.

June 6, 2025
Opened Site B. Found the edge of a stone chest lid by late afternoon — heavier than expected, cracked along one corner but largely intact. Logged as KH-03. We did not open it today; light was failing and I wanted Marcus rested and present for it, ankle or not. He'll never forgive me if we open it without him.

Also: a grey Land Rover was parked at the valley road turnoff this morning that wasn't there yesterday. Tomas didn't recognize it. Probably nothing. Probably.

June 7, 2025
We opened the chest.

Inside, wrapped in oilcloth gone stiff with age, was a bound manuscript — vellum, hand-illuminated, remarkably intact given the damp. Marcus's hands were shaking too badly to photograph it himself, so I did the first documentation pass while he read the opening lines aloud, half in disbelief. The script matches the hand described in the Caldbridge archive catalogue for the lost Thornecliff Manuscript. Logged as KH-04. Sealed it in the humidity-controlled transport case immediately.

I have not felt anything like this since my doctoral fieldwork. I'm trying to stay clinical about it and mostly failing.

June 8, 2025
The grey Land Rover was back, closer this time, parked at the tree line above camp. Tomas walked up and found two people with a spotting scope aimed at our camp — one of them was Priya Nandan. She claimed to be "hiking." I don't believe her, and I don't think she expected me to. Words were exchanged. She left. I've radioed Caldbridge to move up our extraction date and to alert the Ministry contact, Director Okafor's office, that we may need an early escort for the manuscript.

We are packing the transport case tonight rather than waiting for morning.

June 9, 2025
Departed the hollow at first light with KH-04 secured in the transport case, Marcus riding in back with it the entire way down, ankle brace and all. No sign of the grey Land Rover on the valley road. Reached Caldbridge by early afternoon and handed the manuscript directly to the university conservation lab under Ministry seal, per the permit conditions.

Six days. One sprained ankle, one rival collector, one very old book that was not supposed to still exist. I'll write the formal report this week for the Trust. This page, though, is just for me: we found it. After four years of chasing a rumor in a ledger margin, we found it.

— E.W.
